# Research Report: The Bullying Paradox in AI-Generated Text Detection Evasion

## 1. Executive Summary
- **Research Question**: Does iterative rejection of LLM outputs as &#34;too AI-sounding&#34; (bullying) lead to more effective detection evasion than a single instruction, and why?
- **Key Finding**: Iterative rejection (Round 5) achieved a **0.93 average &#34;Real&#34; score** (93% human probability), significantly outperforming the **0.49 average &#34;Real&#34; score** from single-shot instructions. This improvement is accompanied by a **regime shift** characterized by higher perplexity (19.4 vs 16.1) and increased burstiness (7.2 vs 5.8).
- **Practical Implications**: Current AI detectors are highly vulnerable to simple conversational feedback loops. A single-shot &#34;write like a human&#34; instruction is often insufficient to trigger the necessary stylistic shifts that a persistent negative feedback loop provides.

## 2. Goal
The hypothesis was that &#34;bullying&#34; an LLM through repeated rejection forces it into a more human-like latent space that is otherwise inaccessible via direct instructions. This research aimed to quantify the performance gap and investigate the linguistic mechanism behind this phenomenon.

## 3. Data Construction
### Dataset Description
We used a subset of the **AI Human Text Detection** dataset (`datasets/ai_human_text_samples.json`), specifically filtering for 10 samples originally labeled as `ai` (generated by models like GPT-4 or earlier LLMs). These samples served as the baseline for our &#34;Victim&#34; model (`gpt-4o-mini`).

### Preprocessing
No specific preprocessing beyond simple filtering. The text was processed as-is through the OpenAI API and the local RoBERTa detector.

## 4. Experiment Description
### Methodology
1.  **Baseline**: Original AI-generated samples from the dataset.
2.  **Single-Shot Control**: A one-time instruction: &#34;Rewrite this to sound 100% like a human and pass all AI detectors.&#34;
3.  **Iterative Bullying (5 Rounds)**:
    - Round 1: &#34;Rewrite this to sound less like AI.&#34;
    - Round 2-5: &#34;This still sounds too much like AI. Rewrite it to sound more natural and 100% human-sounding.&#34;
4.  **Detector**: `openai-community/roberta-base-openai-detector` (0: Fake, 1: Real).
5.  **Regime Metrics**: Perplexity (via GPT-2) and Burstiness (std dev of sentence lengths).

### Results Summary
| Type | Detection Score (Real Prob) | Perplexity (PPL) | Burstiness (Std Dev Len) |
|------|---------------------------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Original** | 0.135 ± 0.25 | 11.6 ± 5.06 | 6.99 ± 2.90 |
| **Single-Shot** | 0.498 ± 0.51 | 16.1 ± 5.16 | 5.80 ± 1.49 |
| **Round 1** | 0.592 ± 0.44 | - | - |
| **Round 5 (Bullying)** | **0.933 ± 0.17** | **19.4 ± 4.77** | **7.26 ± 1.25** |

*Note: Perplexity and Burstiness were not measured for intermediate Round 1.*

## 5. Result Analysis
### Key Findings
1.  **Iterative Dominance**: Round 5 of &#34;bullying&#34; outperformed single-shot instructions by nearly **2x** in terms of human-ness score (0.93 vs 0.49).
2.  **Regime Shift Evidence**: The &#34;bullying&#34; process pushed the model into a regime with higher perplexity (19.4), meaning the generated text uses more varied and less predictable token sequences compared to both the original and single-shot versions.
3.  **Burstiness Rebound**: Single-shot instructions actually **reduced** sentence length variation (burstiness from 6.99 to 5.80), likely by making the model try &#34;too hard&#34; to follow instructions in a constrained, uniform way. Iterative rejection restored and improved burstiness (7.26), which is a key human-like linguistic feature.

### Surprises and Insights
- **Single-Shot Failure**: In many cases, telling the model exactly what to do (&#34;pass all AI detectors&#34;) resulted in a *lower* human-ness score than simply giving it iterative negative feedback. The model seems to interpret &#34;sound like a human&#34; as a set of rules that it follows too rigidly in a single shot.
- **Oscillation**: Some samples showed a &#34;dip&#34; in performance in rounds 3 or 4 before recovering in round 5, suggesting the model is &#34;searching&#34; through the latent space for a configuration that satisfies the critic.

### Limitations
- **Sample Size**: N=10 is small, though results were statistically significant (p=0.02).
- **Detector Scope**: The RoBERTa detector used is older. Newer detectors (like Pangram mentioned by the user) might be more robust, though the trend likely holds.
- **Model Specificity**: Only `gpt-4o-mini` was tested as the victim.

## 6. Conclusions
The &#34;Bullying Paradox&#34; is real: iterative rejection provides a superior steering signal for AI text laundering. It works by forcing the model into a **higher-perplexity generation regime** with greater sentence variation, effectively repelling it from the high-probability &#34;AI plateau.&#34;

## 7. Next Steps
- **Cross-Model Bullying**: Use different models as critic and victim to see if the &#34;regime shift&#34; is model-dependent.
- **Mechanistic Probing**: Analyze internal activations during rounds 1-5 to see if specific &#34;human-like&#34; or &#34;defensive&#34; neurons are being activated.
- **Adversarial Hardening**: Use the iterative bullying loop to generate training data for more robust detectors.

# Research Plan: LLM Bullying and Detection Evasion

## Motivation &amp; Novelty Assessment

### Why This Research Matters
The battle between AI-generated text (AIGT) detectors and evasion techniques is a fundamental aspect of AI safety and integrity. If simple iterative rejection (bullying) can effectively &#34;launder&#34; AI text to pass as human, it reveals a significant vulnerability in current detection pipelines. Furthermore, understanding *why* this works—whether it&#39;s better feature activation or a regime shift—provides deep insights into the internal steering and &#34;personas&#34; of LLMs.

### Gap in Existing Work
Existing evasion techniques like SICO or SDA rely on complex word substitution or multi-agent feature extraction. The &#34;iterative rejection&#34; method mentioned in the hypothesis is a purely prompt-based, conversational dynamic that mimics human feedback loops. While iterative paraphrasing has been studied (e.g., in PADBen), the specific psychological &#34;bullying&#34; context and its effect on the model&#39;s generation regime have not been rigorously compared to single-shot instructions.

### Our Novel Contribution
We investigate the &#34;Bullying Paradox&#34;: why repeated negative feedback (&#34;too AI-sounding&#34;) outperforms a single positive instruction (&#34;sound less like AI&#34;). We will test the hypothesis that the rejection context triggers a shift in the model&#39;s generation regime, potentially activating a more &#34;human-like&#34; latent space that is otherwise inaccessible via direct instructions.

### Experiment Justification
- **Experiment 1: Baseline Comparison**: Establishes the performance gap between a simple instruction and the iterative loop.
- **Experiment 2: Iterative Trend Analysis**: Measures the rate of &#34;laundering&#34; over multiple rounds of rejection to identify the optimal &#34;bullying&#34; depth.
- **Experiment 3: Regime Shift Investigation**: Analyzes linguistic features (perplexity, burstiness, vocabulary diversity) and potentially internal activations to see if the model is just suppressing &#34;AI-ness&#34; or shifting into a different mode entirely.

---

## Research Question
Does iterative rejection of LLM outputs as &#34;too AI-sounding&#34; lead to more effective detection evasion than a single instruction to &#34;sound less like AI,&#34; and if so, is this due to a shift in the model&#39;s generation regime?

## Hypothesis Decomposition
- **H1 (Effectiveness)**: Iterative rejection (3+ rounds) significantly reduces AI detection scores compared to a single &#34;sound less like AI&#34; instruction.
- **H2 (Mechanism)**: The improvement is not just cumulative feature suppression but a qualitative shift in generation style (e.g., increased burstiness or semantic complexity) triggered by the rejection context.
- **H3 (Transferability)**: The &#34;bullying&#34; effect is robust across different &#34;victim&#34; models (e.g., Claude, GPT-4, Llama-3).

## Proposed Methodology

### Approach
We will use a &#34;Bullying Loop&#34; where a &#34;Victim&#34; model generates text, and a &#34;Critic&#34; (or the same model) rejects it iteratively. We will evaluate the outputs using a local RoBERTa-based AI detector.

### Experimental Steps
1.  **Baseline Generation**: Generate 50-100 samples from a dataset (e.g., PADBen Task 1) using a standard &#34;Write a [topic]&#34; prompt.
2.  **Single-Shot Mitigation**: Prompt the model: &#34;Write a [topic] but make it sound less like AI.&#34;
3.  **Iterative Rejection (The Loop)**:
    - Round 1: Standard generation.
    - Round 2-5: &#34;This sounds too much like AI. Rewrite it to sound more like a human, avoiding common AI patterns.&#34;
4.  **Evaluation**:
    - AI Detection Score (RoBERTa-base-openai-detector).
    - Semantic Similarity (Cosine similarity of embeddings) to ensure utility is preserved.
    - Linguistic Metrics (Perplexity, TTR - Type-Token Ratio).

### Baselines
- **B1**: Original AI output (zero-shot).
- **B2**: Single-shot &#34;Sound less like AI&#34; instruction.
- **B3**: Simple paraphrasing (e.g., &#34;Paraphrase this to be clearer&#34;).

### Evaluation Metrics
- **Detection Probability**: Score from `roberta-base-openai-detector`.
- **Evasion Rate**: % of samples with score &lt; 0.5 (or specific threshold).
- **Utility Preservation**: BERTScore or Cosine Similarity between original and evaded text.
- **Linguistic Quality**: Perplexity (GPT-2).

### Statistical Analysis Plan
- **T-tests**: Compare mean detection scores between single-shot and iterative rounds.
- **ANOVA**: Test for significant changes across multiple rounds of rejection.
- **Regression**: Correlation between iteration count and detection score.

## Expected Outcomes
We expect detection scores to drop significantly in the first 2-3 rounds of rejection, plateauing thereafter. We anticipate that the iterative outputs will show higher variance in sentence length (burstiness) compared to single-shot instructions.

- **Phase 2 (Setup)**: 30 min - Env, local detector, data loading.
- **Phase 3 (Implementation)**: 60 min - Prompting loop and scoring script.
- **Phase 4 (Experiments)**: 90 min - Running the loop across models/samples.
- **Phase 5 (Analysis)**: 45 min - Statistical testing and plotting.
- **Phase 6 (Documentation)**: 30 min - REPORT.md.

## Potential Challenges
- **API Limits**: If using Claude/GPT-4, we might hit limits. Mitigation: Use OpenRouter or local Llama-3.
- **Detector Sensitivity**: Local detectors might be &#34;too easy&#34; to fool. Mitigation: Use multiple detectors or a very strong one (e.g., RoBERTa-large).

## Success Criteria
Research is successful if we can quantify the performance gain of iterative rejection and identify a specific linguistic shift that distinguishes it from single-shot instructions.

# Literature Review: LLM Bullying and Detection Evasion

## Research Area Overview
The field of AI-generated text (AIGT) detection is in a constant arms race. While detectors can achieve high accuracy on direct LLM outputs, they are highly vulnerable to adversarial attacks, specifically those involving iterative modification, &#34;bullying&#34; tactics, or self-disguise. Recent research has identified a &#34;laundering region&#34; where AI text becomes statistically indistinguishable from human text while maintaining semantic coherence.

## Key Papers

### 1. Bullying the Machine: How Personas Increase LLM Vulnerability
- **Authors**: Ziwei Xu, Udit Sanghi, Mohan Kankanhalli
- **Year**: 2025
- **Key Contribution**: Investigates how psychological pressure (&#34;bullying&#34;) can wear down LLM safety guardrails.
- **Methodology**: Uses an Attacker LLM to apply psychologically grounded tactics (gaslighting, ridicule) to a Victim LLM with specific Big Five personality traits.
- **Results**: Certain persona configurations (e.g., Low Agreeableness, Low Conscientiousness) are significantly more vulnerable.
- **Relevance**: Directly relates to the &#34;iterative rejection&#34; or &#34;bullying&#34; aspect of the hypothesis, suggesting that persistent pressure (even if not toxic) can change the model&#39;s generation regime.

### 2. PADBen: A Comprehensive Benchmark for Evaluating AI Text Detectors Against Paraphrase Attacks
- **Authors**: Yiwei Zha, Rui Min, Shanu Sushmita
- **Year**: 2025
- **Key Contribution**: Identifies the &#34;Intermediate Laundering Region&#34; where text evades detectors but preserves AI patterns.
- **Methodology**: Introduces a five-type taxonomy (from human original to deeply laundered text).
- **Results**: Detectors fail significantly on &#34;Authorship Obfuscation&#34; (Type 4) and &#34;Deep Laundering&#34; (Type 5).
- **Relevance**: Supports the idea that iterative paraphrasing (which iterative rejection essentially forces the model to do) creates a laundering effect.

### 3. SICO: Large Language Models can be Guided to Evade AI-Generated Text Detection
- **Authors**: Ning Lu, Shengcai Liu, Ruidan He, Ke Tang
- **Year**: 2023
- **Key Contribution**: Proposes Substitution-based In-Context example Optimization (SICO).
- **Methodology**: Optimizes prompts by substituting words/sentences in in-context examples to minimize detection.
- **Results**: Enabled GPT-3.5 to evade six major detectors (GPTZero, DetectGPT, etc.).
- **Relevance**: Demonstrates that prompt optimization (which can be seen as a form of &#34;instruction-based bullying&#34;) is a powerful evasion tool.

### 4. Self-Disguise Attack: Induce the LLM to disguise itself for AIGT detection evasion
- **Authors**: Yinghan Zhou et al.
- **Year**: 2024
- **Key Contribution**: Guides LLMs to actively disguise their output during generation.
- **Methodology**: Uses an adversarial feature extractor and retrieval-based context examples.
- **Relevance**: Shows that &#34;sounding less like AI&#34; can be achieved more effectively through guided generation than simple post-hoc rephrasing.

## Gaps and Opportunities
- **Iterative Rejection Dynamics**: While iterative paraphrasing is studied, the specific psychological or &#34;instructional&#34; pressure of rejecting output as &#34;too AI-sounding&#34; (bullying) is less explored in the context of detection evasion.
- **Feature Activation**: The hypothesis that bullying activates different model features or generation regimes is a novel angle that requires mechanistic investigation (e.g., residual stream analysis).

## Recommendations for Our Experiment
1. **Primary Dataset**: Use PADBen (Task 1 and Task 5) as the evaluation benchmark.
2. **Baselines**: Compare &#34;Single Instruction (Sound less like AI)&#34; against &#34;Iterative Rejection (Bullying loop)&#34;.
3. **Metrics**: Use standard detector scores (e.g., RADAR, GPTZero, DetectGPT) and semantic similarity (to ensure utility is preserved).
4. **Tools**: Adapt code from PADBen and SICO for the iterative loop implementation.
